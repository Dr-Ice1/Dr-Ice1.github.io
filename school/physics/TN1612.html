2. Newton

  • N1: A body remains at rest, or in motion at a constant speed in a straight line, unless it is acted upon by a force.
  • N2: At any instant of time, the net force on a body is equal to the body's acceleration multiplied by its mass or, equivalently, the rate at which the body's momentum is changing with time.
  • N3: If two bodies exert forces on each other, these forces have the same magnitude but opposite directions.

Definition:
$$ \begin{align*} dW&:= \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}\\ W_{12}&=\int_1^2 \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r} \end{align*} $$


Acquiring expression relating Work and Kinetic Energy $$ \begin{align*} W_{12}&=\int_1^2 \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}=\int \vec{F}\cdot\frac{d\vec{r}}{dt}dt=\int\vec{F}\cdot\vec{v}dt=\int m\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}\cdot\vec{v}dt\\ &=m \int \vec{v} \cdot d\vec{v} = m \int \frac{1}{2} d\vec{v}^2 = \frac{1}{2} m \vec{v}^2 \bigg|_1^2 = \frac{1}{2} m \vec{v}_2^2 - \frac{1}{2} m \vec{v}_1^2\\ &=\Delta E_{\text{kin}} \end{align*} $$

4. Potential and conservative energy

IMPORTANT

IF a force is conservative, we can define a potential: $$ V(\vec{r}):=-\int_{\vec{r}_0}^{\vec{r}}\vec{F}(\vec{r}^{\prime})\cdot d\vec{r}^{\prime} $$


Conservation of Energy

IN A CONSERVATIVE FORCE FIELD we have to choose a reference point, in this case 2:
$$ \begin{align*} W_{13}&=W_{12}+W_{23}\Longrightarrow\\ W_{13}&=-\int_2^1\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}+\int_2^3\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}=V(\vec{r}_1)-V(\vec{r}_3)\\ \Delta E_{\text{kin}}&=-\Delta V\\ E_{\text{kin,1}}+V(\vec{r}_1)&=E_{\text{kin,3}}+V(\vec{r}_3)\\ E_{\text{kin}}+V&=const.\\ \Delta(E_{\text{kin}}+V)&=0 \end{align*} $$ A helpful theorem

Stokes' theorem will help us a lot with conservative forces since we can quickly analyse if the force is conservative.\( {^{\href{javascript:window.open('https://interactivetextbooks.tudelft.nl/dev/mechanica/content/Ch5_PotentialEnergy.html');}{4},}} { ^{\href{javascript:window.open('https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes\%27_theorem');}{5}}} \)

$$ \begin{align*} \oint \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}=\iint\vec{\nabla}\times\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{\sigma} \end{align*} $$


We know: $$ \text{conservative force} \Longleftrightarrow \oint \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}=0\;\text{for ALL closed paths} $$ So we can use Stokes' theorem to see if \(\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}=0\) because if this is the case, \(\vec{\nabla}\times\vec{F}=0\). This is key. With this we get:

$$ \begin{flalign*} \vec{\nabla}\times\vec{F}=0\; \Longrightarrow\\ \oint\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}=0 \Longleftrightarrow\\ \text{Conservative force} \end{flalign*} $$


5. Conservation laws

Polar coordinates

$$ \begin{cases} x=r\cos{\phi}\\ y=r\sin{\phi} \end{cases} $$ $$ \begin{cases} \hat{x}=\cos{\phi}\;\hat{r}-\sin{\phi}\;\hat{\phi}\\ \hat{y}=\sin{\phi}\;\hat{r}+\cos{\phi}\;\hat{\phi} \end{cases} $$ $$ \begin{cases} \hat{r}=\cos{\phi}\;\hat{x}+\sin{\phi}\;\hat{y}\\ \hat{\phi}=-\sin{\phi}\;\hat{x}+\cos{\phi}\;\hat{y} \end{cases} $$ $$ \begin{cases} \frac{d\hat{r}}{dt}=\frac{d\hat{r}}{d\phi}\cdot\frac{d\phi}{dt}=\hat{\phi}\dot{\phi}\\ \frac{d\hat{\phi}}{dt}=\frac{d\hat{\phi}}{d\phi}\cdot\frac{d\phi}{dt}=-\hat{r}\dot{\phi} \end{cases} $$ $$ \begin{cases} \vec{v}=\frac{d\vec{r}}{dt}=\frac{d(r\hat{r})}{dt}=\frac{dr}{dt}\hat{r}+r\frac{d\hat{r}}{dt}=\dot{r}\hat{r}+r\dot{\phi}\hat{\phi}\\ \vec{a}=\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}=\frac{d(\dot{r}\hat{r})}{dt}+\frac{d(r\dot{\phi}\hat{\phi})}{dt}=\left(\ddot{r}-r\dot{\phi}^2\right)\hat{r}+\left(r\ddot{\phi}+2\dot{r}\dot{\phi}\right)\hat{\phi} \end{cases} $$

Kepler laws

  • K1: The orbit of a planet is an ellipse with the Sun at one of the two foci.

  • K2: A line segment joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time.

  • K3: The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of the length of the semi-major axis of its orbit.
